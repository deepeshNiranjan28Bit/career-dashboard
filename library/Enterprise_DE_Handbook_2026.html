How Enterprise DE Projects Actually Run

Before any architecture, understand the machine you operate inside: who pays, who decides, who you sit with, and the phases every project moves through. Interviewers can tell in 30 seconds whether you've lived this.

Two worlds: consulting delivery vs product engineering

In a services/consulting firm (TCS, Infosys, Accenture, Deloitte, EPAM, Thoughtworks, and the boutiques), you are billed to a client. There is a Statement of Work (SOW), a contract, milestones, and someone tracking your utilization. Delivery is organized as a pod/squad: a delivery manager, a solution/data architect, a lead engineer, 3–6 data engineers, a QA/test engineer, sometimes a BA (business analyst) and a scrum master. Work is usually split onshore/offshore — onshore engineers/BAs sit with the client for requirements and demos; offshore (often India) does the bulk of the build across a time-zone handoff.

In a product company (a bank's own data platform team, a SaaS firm, a fintech), you build for internal stakeholders — analysts, data scientists, product managers, finance, compliance. No SOW, but there are OKRs, roadmaps, on-call rotations, and platform SLAs. The engineering rigor is often higher and longer-lived; you own what you build for years.

The lifecycle below is nearly identical in both; the vocabulary differs (SOW/milestone vs epic/OKR).

Every project in this book is documented against this spine. Learn it once; reuse it forever.

Discovery / Assessment. Understand the client's business, current state, pain, data sources, volumes, and success criteria. Output: a current-state assessment and a target-state proposal. This is where "reports take 10 hours" gets quantified into money.

Requirements. Functional (what the system must do) and non-functional (how well — latency, volume, availability, security, cost). Captured by BAs/architects with source-system SMEs. Output: a signed requirements/BRD + source-to-target mappings (STM).

Design. HLD (High-Level Design) is the architecture: components, data flow, technology choices, security zones, environments — the picture an architect signs off. LLD (Low-Level Design) is the buildable detail: table schemas, exact transformations, SCD strategy, partitioning, job dependencies, error handling, naming. Output: HLD + LLD documents reviewed in an Architecture Review Board (ARB).

Build. Engineers implement in sprints (2 weeks), demoing increments. Code goes through PRs, CI, and dev/QA environments. This is 50–70% of the timeline.

Test. Unit (dev), integration (pipeline end-to-end), system, performance/volume, and data reconciliation testing (does the new platform match the legacy numbers to the penny?). Reconciliation is the make-or-break test in migrations.

UAT. Business users validate against real scenarios in a UAT environment. Sign-off here is the gate to production.

Go-live / Cutover. The controlled switch to production — often a parallel run (old + new in production simultaneously) until the new platform is trusted, then decommission the old. Cutover runbooks, rollback plans, and a "war room" are standard.

Hypercare. 2–4 weeks of heightened support right after go-live: the build team stays on to fix issues fast before handing to run/BAU.

BAU / Run. Steady-state operation: on-call, monitoring, incident management, enhancements, cost reviews. This is where your Jio production-support experience lives — and it's the phase most candidates can't speak to.

Who you actually work with (RACI in plain English)

  • Delivery / Engagement Manager — owns the client relationship, timeline, budget, staffing. You escalate blockers here.
  • Solution / Data Architect — owns HLD, technology decisions, and the ARB. You bring LLD and trade-offs to them.
  • Tech Lead / Lead DE — owns the LLD, code standards, PR reviews, and unblocks the team. Often your day-to-day manager.
  • Data Engineers (you) — build pipelines, tests, IaC, and fix incidents.
  • Business Analyst — translates business needs into requirements and source-to-target mappings.
  • QA / Test Engineer — writes test cases, runs reconciliation, signs off quality.
  • Source-system SMEs / DBAs — own the source (Oracle, mainframe, Salesforce); you need them for schemas, extracts, and CDC.
  • Product Owner / Business stakeholder — prioritizes the backlog, accepts demos, signs UAT.
  • Data Governance / Security / Compliance — approve PII handling, masking, access, and (in banking/health) regulatory controls.
  • Platform / Cloud / DevOps team — own the landing zone, networking, Terraform baselines, and shared services.
  • Data Stewards / Analysts / Data Scientists — your downstream consumers; their trust is the real success metric.

Agile in data projects (the honest version)

Most teams run Scrum (2-week sprints, standups, backlog refinement, sprint review/demo, retro) or Kanban for run/BAU. Work is tracked in Jira/Azure Boards as epics → stories → tasks. Data work is lumpier than app work — a single "ingest 40 source tables" story hides discovery, schema surprises, and reconciliation, so estimates slip. Mature teams break work by source system and medallion layer, and treat a table as "done" only when it's ingested, transformed, tested, reconciled, and documented — not just when the code runs.

Say-it-cold: "We ran 2-week sprints; I picked up stories from the backlog in Jira, built the ingestion/transform in a feature branch, raised a PR that ran CI (lint, unit tests, GE data checks), promoted through dev→QA, and the story was done only after reconciliation passed and the runbook was updated. Every fortnight we demoed increments to the product owner."

Swap the boxes for the AWS flavour (S3 + Glue/EMR + Redshift), the GCP flavour (GCS + Dataproc/Dataflow + BigQuery), or the Snowflake-centric flavour (Snowpipe + Streams/Tasks + dbt) and the shape is the same: land raw, refine in layers, serve to BI/ML, orchestrate + govern + secure + observe around it.

Medallion, everywhere

Bronze = raw landing, schema-on-read, immutable, append-only, partitioned by ingest date — your replay source of truth. Silver = cleaned, deduplicated, conformed, typed, with data-quality checks and business keys resolved — the "single version of truth" tables. Gold = business-ready marts/aggregates/features, dimensional models, serving BI and ML. Each layer is a Delta table; each is independently reprocessable; failures are contained to a layer. In interviews, always frame your pipeline in these three layers — it signals you've built real platforms.

Repository strategy

One repo per project/domain is the common default; large platforms use a mono-repo with per-domain folders. Branching: trunk-based or GitFlow-lite — main (prod), short-lived feature/* branches, PR + review + CI to merge, tags for releases. Notebooks live in Databricks Repos synced to Git; prod logic is packaged code (wheels), not notebooks.

The security baseline (assume this on every project)

  • Identity: Managed Identity / Service Principals for job auth; users via SSO (Azure AD/Okta) + SCIM; no shared creds.
  • Secrets: Key Vault / Databricks secret scopes / HashiCorp Vault — never in code or config files.
  • Access: RBAC + least privilege; Unity Catalog grants at catalog/schema/table/row/column level; ABAC/row filters for multi-tenant.
  • Network: Private Endpoints / VNet injection, no public storage, firewall rules, NSGs; data never traverses the public internet.
  • Data protection: encryption at rest (platform-managed or CMK) + in transit (TLS); PII masking/tokenization; column-level encryption for sensitive fields.
  • Governance: catalog + lineage (Unity Catalog/Purview), PII classification, retention policies, audit logging.
  • Compliance overlays: banking (RBI, PCI-DSS, SOX), health (HIPAA/HITECH), EU (GDPR), each adding masking, residency, audit, and retention rules.

Project 1 — Real-Time Fraud Detection Pipeline

BankingStreaming

A tier-1 retail bank losing money to card-not-present fraud needs to score every transaction in under a second and block bad ones before authorization completes.

Business Background & Industry

Client: a large retail bank (25M cards, ~3,000 transactions/second at peak). Card fraud — especially card-not-present (online) — was costing an estimated ₹180 crore/year in write-offs and chargebacks, plus regulatory scrutiny and customer churn after fraud events. Their existing fraud system was a nightly batch that flagged fraud after money had already moved, so recovery was rare.

Why they approached us: the batch model gave zero real-time protection, false-positive rates were high (blocking genuine customers, driving call-center cost), and the rules engine was a black box only two people understood. They wanted sub-second scoring at authorization time, a feature store the data-science team could evolve, and full auditability for the regulator.

The money framing (say this in interviews): "Every 100ms of added authorization latency measurably drops approval rates, and every fraudulent transaction that slips through is a direct loss plus a chargeback fee. So the NFRs — p99 < 300ms end-to-end and 99.99% availability — literally were the business case."

Existing Problems / Current Architecture

Card switch ──► Core banking (mainframe) ──► nightly extract ──► Oracle ──► SAS batch rules ──► fraud analyst queue (next day)

Failure modes: detection lagged the fraud by 12–24 hours; the SAS rules were static and couldn't use behavioural features; no replay or lineage; scaling meant buying more mainframe MIPS. Analysts drowned in false positives with no feedback loop to improve rules.

Functional Requirements

  • Score every authorization request in real time and return allow/deny/step-up.
  • Compute behavioural features: velocity (txns per card per minute/hour), spend deviation vs customer baseline, geo-impossible travel, merchant risk, device/IP reputation.
  • Support both a rules engine and an ML model, with champion/challenger.
  • Feed flagged cases to an analyst case-management UI with full context.
  • Capture analyst dispositions as labels to retrain models (feedback loop).
  • Provide auditable lineage: for any decision, reproduce the exact features and model version used.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Latency: p99 scoring < 300ms; end-to-end auth budget ~500ms.
  • Throughput: 3k TPS sustained, 8k peak; horizontally scalable.
  • Availability: 99.99% (fail-open vs fail-closed policy defined with risk).
  • Consistency/Exactly-once: no double-scoring, no lost transactions.
  • Security: PCI-DSS scope, PAN tokenized, network isolation, full audit.
  • Retention: 7-year audit; 90-day hot features.

High-Level Design (HLD)

Card switch/auth ─► Kafka (txn topic) ─► Stream processor (Structured Streaming on Databricks)
                                              │  1. enrich w/ feature store (Redis online + Delta offline)
                                              │  2. compute real-time velocity features (stateful, watermarked)
                                              │  3. score: rules + ML model (MLflow-served, low-latency)
                                              ▼
                              decision topic ─► auth response (allow/deny/step-up)
                                              │
   Kafka ─► Autoloader ─► BRONZE (raw txn Delta) ─► SILVER (cleaned/enriched) ─► GOLD (features, case marts)
                                              │                                        │
                              analyst case UI ◄── flagged cases                Snowflake/Power BI (fraud KPIs, model perf)
   Feedback: analyst dispositions ─► labels ─► retrain (Databricks + MLflow) ─► deploy new model version

Two coupled paths: a hot path (Kafka → streaming scorer → decision, all in-memory, sub-300ms) and a cold path (everything also lands in the lakehouse for features, analytics, retraining, and audit). This lambda-ish split is classic in fraud: you cannot put a lakehouse in the 300ms budget, so the online store (Redis) serves features the batch layer precomputes.

Low-Level Design (LLD)

Streaming scorer

Structured Streaming reads the txn topic with trigger(processingTime="200ms") style micro-batches (or continuous). Stateful aggregations (velocity) keyed by card_id with a watermark of a few minutes to bound state; state stored in RocksDB state store with checkpointing to ADLS for exactly-once recovery. Online features fetched from Redis by card/merchant key; model invoked in-process (model packaged with the job, or a low-latency MLflow endpoint) to avoid network hops.

Feature store

Offline features computed in silver/gold Delta (batch, hourly/daily baselines — 30/90-day spend, typical geos/merchants). A sync job pushes the latest per-key features to Redis (online store) so the hot path reads them in <5ms. Databricks Feature Store keeps offline/online in sync and guarantees train/serve parity (same feature definitions).

Delta tables
  • bronze.txn_raw — partitioned by ingest_dt, append-only, audit cols.
  • silver.txn_enriched — deduped on txn_id (MERGE), typed, joined to card/customer/merchant dims.
  • gold.card_features_daily — behavioural baselines; gold.fraud_case — flagged cases; gold.model_decision — every decision with feature snapshot + model version (audit).
Error handling

Malformed/late events → dead-letter topic + quarantine Delta table. Redis miss → fall back to conservative default features + flag "degraded scoring". Model timeout → configurable fail-open (approve, log) or fail-closed (step-up) per risk policy.

Complete Data Flow

Source & cadence: the card switch emits an ISO-8583-derived JSON/Avro auth message per transaction, continuously, ~3k/s. Ingest: Kafka (Avro + Schema Registry) is the front door; the streaming job is a consumer group. Hot path: enrich → feature lookup → score → publish decision, all within ~150–250ms. Cold path: the same Kafka topic is also consumed by Autoloader into bronze.txn_raw (micro-batch, ~1 min); hourly jobs build silver/gold; daily jobs refresh baselines and push to Redis. Feedback: analyst dispositions from the case UI land via API → bronze → labels → weekly retraining in Databricks, registered in MLflow, promoted champion/challenger.

Security: PCI-DSS scope minimized by tokenizing PAN at the edge (only tokens flow downstream); VNet-injected Databricks, Private Endpoints to storage/Redis, no public egress; Unity Catalog column masking on PII; every decision logged immutably for audit; secrets in Key Vault via Managed Identity.

Infra: Terraform provisions the workspace, job clusters (Photon-enabled, autoscaling 4–40 workers), Redis, Kafka/Event Hub, storage, Key Vault, networking. Job clusters (not all-purpose) for the streaming job with a fixed floor for latency stability.

Deployment/Cutover: shadow mode first (score in parallel, don't act), then canary on a small card segment, then full — with fail-open policy and instant rollback to the previous model version.

Prod support: 24×7 on-call; runbooks for lag spikes, Redis outage (degraded mode), model regression, and checkpoint corruption. RTO 15 min, RPO ~0 (replay from Kafka).

Cost optimization: right-sized streaming cluster with autoscaling floor; batch feature jobs on spot/ephemeral job clusters; Delta OPTIMIZE + Z-ORDER on card_id; VACUUM with 7-day retention; tiered storage for >90-day audit; Redis sized to hot key set only.

Common Production Issues

Kafka consumer lag spikes at peak medium

Cause: scorer can't keep up (skewed hot cards, GC pauses, Redis latency). Fix: increase partitions + parallelism, tune micro-batch size, cache Redis reads, scale executors; long-term, isolate hot merchants. Watch lag as the primary SLO signal.

State store / checkpoint corruption after a bad deploy hard

Cause: incompatible code change to stateful logic. Fix: never change state schema in place — start a new checkpoint location and reprocess from a safe Kafka offset; keep the old job running until the new one is caught up (blue/green streaming).

Redis outage → feature misses medium

Fail into degraded scoring (conservative defaults + step-up), alert immediately, and flag those decisions for review. Never fail-closed globally on a cache outage — it would decline the whole bank.

Model drift / rising false positives medium

Monitor score distribution and precision/recall against analyst labels; trigger retraining; use champion/challenger so a new model proves itself in shadow before promotion. Roll back instantly via MLflow if a promotion regresses.

  • Designed an online/offline feature store (Redis + Delta Feature Store) guaranteeing train/serve parity and reproducible, auditable decisions via Delta time travel.
  • Implemented exactly-once streaming with RocksDB state + checkpointing, DLQ handling, and blue/green streaming deployments with instant model rollback via MLflow.

Interview Q&A

Why not just run the model on the lakehouse directly? medium

A lakehouse query can't meet a single-digit-millisecond feature read or a 300ms budget. So batch precomputes features into an online store (Redis) and the hot path only does key lookups + in-process scoring. The lakehouse handles everything not on the critical path: features, analytics, retraining, audit.

How do you guarantee exactly-once here? hard

Kafka offsets committed atomically with the checkpoint; idempotent decision publishing keyed by txn_id; MERGE/dedup into Delta on txn_id for the cold path. A crash replays from the last checkpoint without double-acting.

Fail-open or fail-closed? hard

A risk decision, not an engineering one — documented with the business. Typically fail-open (approve + flag) for availability on infra failures, but step-up (OTP) for high-risk segments. The point in an interview is to show you know it's a policy trade-off you surface, not silently choose.

Project 2 — Regulatory Reporting Platform

BankingBatch · Governed

A bank must submit accurate, fully-auditable regulatory returns (RBI/Basel/AML) on fixed deadlines. Wrong or late numbers mean fines and reputational damage — correctness and lineage beat speed.

Business Background & Industry

Client: a commercial bank filing dozens of periodic regulatory returns (capital adequacy, liquidity/LCR, AML/CTR/STR, large-exposure). Reports were assembled by finance analysts pulling from a dozen systems into Excel with manual adjustments — error-prone, slow, and impossible to audit ("where did this number come from?"). A single restatement to the regulator triggers investigations and penalties.

Why they approached us: a regulator observation flagged data-lineage and reproducibility gaps. They needed an automated, governed platform where every reported figure traces back to source records, with controls, sign-offs, and versioned submissions.

Framing: "Here the NFR that dominates isn't latency — it's correctness, auditability, and controlled change. The architecture is optimized so any number can be drilled back to source, and any run can be reproduced exactly."

Existing Problems / Current Architecture

Core banking + cards + treasury + AML systems ──► ad-hoc extracts ──► Oracle staging ──► Excel + manual adjustments ──► PDF to regulator

Problems: no single source of truth, manual adjustments with no audit, reconciliation done by eye, no reproducibility, key-person dependency, and month-end crunch where analysts worked nights.

Functional Requirements

  • Ingest from all contributing source systems on a defined schedule (daily/monthly).
  • Apply regulatory business rules/classifications transparently and versioned.
  • Produce each return in the regulator's exact template/format, on deadline.
  • Support controlled manual adjustments with maker-checker approval and full audit.
  • Provide drill-down lineage from any reported cell to source transactions.
  • Version and archive every submission (what was filed, when, by whom, from which data).

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Correctness: reconciliation to source to the last rupee; no silent drops.
  • Auditability: immutable lineage + time travel; 7–10 year retention.
  • Availability: must complete before the filing deadline (hard SLA), with backfill/rerun.
  • Security & segregation of duties: maker-checker, RBAC, SOX-style controls.
  • Change control: rule changes go through review + sign-off + versioning.

LLD

Silver builds conformed dimensions (customer, account, GL, product) as SCD Type 2 so a report reproduces the classification that was valid on the reporting date. Reg rules are implemented as versioned, tested transformations (dbt models or PySpark modules) — each rule has a rule_id, effective dates, and unit tests. Manual adjustments are rows in an adjustments Delta table with maker, checker, reason, and approval status; nothing is edited in place. Every run is tagged with a run_id and the input Delta versions, so time travel reproduces it exactly. Gold marts feed a templating layer that emits the regulator's format (fixed-width/XML/XBRL).

HLD — the migration approach

1. ASSESS  ── inventory tables, ETL jobs, reports, dependencies, data volumes, SLAs (automated scanners + interviews)
2. DESIGN  ── target lakehouse model (bronze/silver/gold), map Teradata objects → Delta, ETL → PySpark/dbt
3. MIGRATE DATA ── one-time history load (bulk export → ADLS → Delta) + ongoing incremental/CDC until cutover
4. CONVERT ETL  ── Informatica/BTEQ → PySpark/dbt (automated conversion tools + manual refactor)
5. RECONCILE    ── row counts, aggregates, column checksums: legacy vs lakehouse, per table, per report
6. PARALLEL RUN ── run both in production, compare outputs daily until trust threshold met

Data Flow & LLD highlights

History load: bulk-export Teradata (TPT/Parquet) to ADLS, load to bronze, build silver/gold to match legacy marts. Incremental: CDC or watermark extracts keep the lakehouse current during the (often months-long) parallel run. ETL conversion: map each Informatica mapping/BTEQ to a tested PySpark/dbt model; recreate SCD logic, surrogate keys, and business rules faithfully. Reconciliation harness: automated per-table row counts, aggregate sums per key column, and hash/checksum comparisons; a dashboard tracks parity % and open breaks. Dialect gotchas: Teradata vs Spark SQL differences (implicit casts, NULL ordering, date handling, QUALIFY, recursive queries) are a top source of subtle mismatches — catalogued and handled.

Databricks — Deep Dive

The platform you'll spend most days in. Know every object, when to use it, and — critically — what a Data Engineer actually does inside it day to day.

What it is & where it sits

Databricks is a managed lakehouse platform built on Spark + Delta, running on your cloud (Azure/AWS/GCP). It sits in the middle of the reference architecture: it's the compute + transformation + ML engine that reads raw data from the lake (ADLS/S3), refines it through bronze→silver→gold Delta tables, and is governed by Unity Catalog. It replaces a pile of separate tools (Spark cluster + notebooks + job scheduler + ML platform + catalog) with one.

The objects — and when to use each

  • Workspace — the environment (notebooks, repos, jobs, clusters). Usually one per env (dev/qa/prod) or per business unit.
  • All-purpose (interactive) cluster — for development, exploration, shared notebooks. Stays up; costs more. Never run production jobs on these.
  • Job cluster — spun up for a scheduled job, torn down after. Cheaper, isolated, reproducible. This is what prod pipelines use.
  • Cluster policies — guardrails admins set (allowed node types, autoscaling limits, tags, Photon, auto-termination) so teams can't spin up runaway cost. Every enterprise uses these.
  • Photon — the vectorized C++ execution engine; big speedups for SQL/DataFrame workloads. Turn on for BI-heavy and large ETL.
  • Autoscaling — cluster grows/shrinks with load; set a sensible floor for streaming latency stability and a ceiling for cost.
  • Notebooks — for development, exploration, and orch-called tasks. Prod logic should be packaged code (wheels), with notebooks as thin entrypoints.
  • Repos — Git-backed folders; how code is version-controlled and promoted. Notebooks sync to GitHub/Azure DevOps.
  • Jobs / Workflows — the native orchestrator: multi-task DAGs with dependencies, retries, alerts, schedules, and parameters. Often used instead of/with Airflow.
  • Delta Live Tables (DLT) — declarative pipeline framework: you declare tables + expectations, DLT manages dependencies, incremental processing, data quality, and auto-scaling. Great for medallion pipelines with less boilerplate.
  • Unity Catalog — governance: catalog.schema.table namespace, grants, column masking, row filters, lineage, audit. (Own deep-dive section.)
  • Volumes — UC-governed storage for non-tabular files (models, images, exports) with the same access control as tables.
  • Libraries — packages (wheels/JARs/PyPI) installed on clusters; managed via policies/init scripts for reproducibility.
  • Secrets — secret scopes (backed by Key Vault) for credentials — never hardcode.
  • DBFS — the (legacy) file system abstraction; in UC-first setups prefer Volumes + external locations over DBFS root.
  • Feature Store — offline/online feature tables with train/serve parity (used in the fraud/rec projects).
  • MLflow — experiment tracking + model registry + serving.

Delta optimization — the levers you must know

  • Partitioning — physical split by a low/medium-cardinality column (usually date) for pruning. Don't over-partition (small files).
  • OPTIMIZE — bin-pack small files into larger ones; fixes the small-files problem.
  • Z-ORDER — multi-dimensional clustering on filter columns to improve data skipping via min/max stats.
  • Liquid Clustering — newer alternative to partitioning + Z-order that adapts automatically; less manual tuning.
  • VACUUM — physically delete unreferenced files older than retention (default 7 days). Reclaims storage but limits time travel — never shorten retention casually.
  • Auto Optimize / Auto Compaction — write-time compaction so streaming doesn't create file explosions.
  • Caching — Delta cache (SSD) for hot reads; Spark cache for reused DataFrames (only if reused).
  • Checkpointing — streaming state/offset location for exactly-once recovery; treat as sacred (don't delete/change casually).
  • MERGE — the workhorse for upserts, CDC apply, SCD2, and idempotent writes.
  • CDF (Change Data Feed) — emit row-level changes for incremental downstream consumption.

What you ACTUALLY do daily as a DE in Databricks

A realistic day: pull a story from Jira → open your Repo branch → develop the transform in a notebook against a small sample on a dev all-purpose cluster → refactor logic into the packaged common library (functions, schema, DQ) → write/run pytest unit tests locally and Great Expectations checks → wire it into a Workflow (or DLT) with the right dependencies, retries, and a job cluster spec → raise a PR (CI runs lint/tests) → promote to QA, validate reconciliation → check the Spark UI when a job is slow (find the skewed stage, add a broadcast or repartition) → run OPTIMIZE/Z-ORDER on the output → update the runbook/data dictionary. On-call days: triage an alert (lag, failure, DQ break), drill via lineage, fix root cause, backfill the affected partition idempotently. You spend far more time on correctness, tests, tuning, and operations than on writing new Spark code.

Interview gold: "In Databricks I keep prod logic as packaged wheels, not notebooks; run it on job clusters governed by cluster policies with Photon and autoscaling; orchestrate with Workflows/DLT; enforce quality with Great Expectations; and keep Delta healthy with partitioning, OPTIMIZE/Z-ORDER, and VACUUM. I debug via the Spark UI and Unity Catalog lineage."

Interview Q&A

Job cluster vs all-purpose cluster — when and why? medium

All-purpose for dev/interactive (stays on, shared, costlier); job cluster for scheduled production (ephemeral, isolated, cheaper, reproducible). Running prod on all-purpose clusters wastes money and risks noisy-neighbor interference.

DLT vs hand-written Workflows? medium

DLT is declarative — you define tables + expectations and it manages dependencies, incremental processing, quality, and scaling. It reduces boilerplate for medallion pipelines. Hand-written Workflows give more control for complex/custom logic. Many teams mix: DLT for standard flows, Workflows for orchestration and non-DLT tasks.

How do you fix the small-files problem? medium

Right-size writes (coalesce/repartition), enable Auto Compaction/Optimize Writes for streaming, run scheduled OPTIMIZE, and partition sensibly (not high-cardinality). Consider Liquid Clustering to avoid manual partition/Z-order tuning.

Why package code as wheels instead of running notebooks in prod? hard

Wheels are versioned, unit-testable, reusable across jobs, and CI/CD-friendly; notebooks-in-prod are hard to test, review, and reuse and encourage copy-paste. Notebooks become thin entrypoints that call the library.

Snowflake — Deep Dive

The serving warehouse in most stacks. Know the architecture, every object, and — the thing interviewers push on — cost.

Architecture recap & where it sits

Three decoupled layers: cloud services (metadata, optimizer, security), compute (virtual warehouses), and storage (central, columnar, compressed micro-partitions). It sits at the serve layer — gold marts land here for BI/analyst self-serve — or, increasingly, as the transform engine too (Streams/Tasks/Snowpipe + dbt). Its superpower is elastic, isolated compute over one copy of data.

The objects — and what they're for

  • Database / Schema — logical containers (often per domain + env); tables/views live here.
  • Virtual Warehouse — a resizable compute cluster (XS→6XL). Size for query complexity; multi-cluster for concurrency. Auto-suspend + auto-resume are the #1 cost control.
  • Stages — pointers to files for load/unload: internal (Snowflake-managed) or external (ADLS/S3). Source for COPY/Snowpipe.
  • Snowpipe — continuous, event-triggered micro-batch ingestion from a stage (near-real-time file loading).
  • Streams — change tracking (CDC) on a table: what rows changed since last consumed. Pairs with Tasks for incremental ELT.
  • Tasks — scheduled SQL (or DAGs of tasks); combine with Streams for in-warehouse pipelines (stream→task→MERGE).
  • Time Travel — query/restore data up to N days back (1 standard, up to 90 Enterprise) — undo mistakes, audit.
  • Fail-safe — extra 7-day Snowflake-managed recovery (DR only, not self-serve).
  • Zero-Copy Clone — instant metadata-only copies (dev/test/QA from prod) with copy-on-write; no storage until divergence.
  • Data Sharing / Marketplace — share live data with other accounts without copying (secure data sharing).
  • RBAC — role hierarchy; grants on databases/schemas/objects; roles assigned to users/services.
  • Micro-partitions & Clustering keys — automatic columnar chunks with min/max metadata for pruning; clustering keys help huge tables.
  • Result cache & warehouse cache — identical queries free for 24h; local SSD cache speeds repeats.
  • VARIANT — semi-structured (JSON/Avro/Parquet) column with path querying + FLATTEN.

Cost optimization (the interview magnet)

  • Auto-suspend idle warehouses (seconds), auto-resume on demand — idle warehouses are the classic bill shock.
  • Right-size warehouses to the workload; scale up for big queries, out (multi-cluster) for concurrency — don't leave everything on XL.
  • Separate warehouses per workload (ELT vs BI vs ad-hoc) to isolate cost and contention.
  • Reduce bytes scanned: pruning via good clustering, avoid SELECT *, prune partitions/filters.
  • Leverage result cache; batch loads via COPY vs many tiny loads; use Snowpipe only where near-real-time is needed.
  • Monitor with ACCOUNT_USAGE / warehouse credit dashboards; set resource monitors + alerts on credit thresholds.

What you do daily in Snowflake as a DE

Model and build gold marts (often via dbt running on Snowflake), manage COPY/Snowpipe loads from stages, build Stream+Task incremental pipelines, tune warehouse sizing and clustering for cost/perf, set RBAC grants and masking policies, spin zero-copy clones for testing, and watch credit consumption. You optimize queries by reading the query profile (pruning %, spilled to remote, exploding joins) and adjusting clustering/warehouse/SQL.

Interview gold: "Snowflake gives me elastic isolated compute over one copy of data. I control cost with auto-suspend, right-sizing, and per-workload warehouses; build incremental ELT with Streams+Tasks; test safely with zero-copy clones; and tune via the query profile (pruning, spilling) and clustering keys."

Interview Q&A

How would you cut a runaway Snowflake bill? medium

Find idle/oversized warehouses (ACCOUNT_USAGE), enable aggressive auto-suspend, right-size and split warehouses by workload, add resource monitors, reduce bytes scanned via clustering/pruning and killing SELECT *, and prefer batch COPY over excessive Snowpipe. Measure credits before/after.

Streams + Tasks — how do they build a pipeline? medium

A Stream captures changes on a source table; a scheduled Task reads the Stream and MERGEs changes into a target — an in-warehouse incremental ELT loop without external orchestration. Chain Tasks into a DAG for multi-step flows.

When Snowflake vs Databricks for transformation? hard

Snowflake for SQL-centric ELT, BI serving, and semi-structured with minimal ops; Databricks for heavy Spark/ML/streaming and open lakehouse. Many stacks do heavy engineering in Databricks/Delta and serve BI from Snowflake — though both increasingly overlap.

CI/CD for Data Engineering — End to End

How code actually goes from your laptop to production in an enterprise. Being able to narrate this pipeline is a strong senior signal.

The full flow

Developer commits ─► Git (feature branch)
        │
   Pull Request ─► Peer Review + automated checks:
        ├─ Lint / format (ruff, black)
        ├─ SonarQube (code quality, security, coverage gate)
        ├─ Unit tests (pytest) — transform logic on fixtures

What each stage actually does

  • Feature branch + PR — isolates change; PR is where review + CI happen. Trunk-based keeps branches short.
  • Lint/format — consistency, catch trivial errors before review.
  • SonarQube — quality gate: fails the build on bugs, code smells, security hotspots, or low coverage. Enforces standards no one can skip.
  • Unit tests — pure transform functions on small fixtures incl. edge cases (nulls, dupes, empty). Fast, run on every push.
  • Data tests — GE/dbt assertions (uniqueness, not-null, referential, ranges) so bad data logic is caught in CI.

  • Deploy — push the artifact + job/DAG definitions to the target env's compute.
  • Integration tests — run the pipeline end-to-end on representative sample data; validate outputs + reconciliation.
  • Promotion gates — QA/UAT sign-offs; PROD requires change management (ticket, approval, window) and is fully automated (no manual edits).
  • Rollback — redeploy the previous artifact version; Terraform reverts infra; Delta/DB migrations designed to be reversible.
Interview gold: "Every change is a PR that runs lint, SonarQube, unit + data tests, and builds a versioned wheel. Merged code triggers CD: Terraform provisions infra, the exact artifact is promoted dev→QA→UAT→prod with tests and approvals at each gate, and prod deploys are tied to a change ticket. Rollback is redeploying the prior artifact version — we never hand-edit prod."

Interview Q&A

How do you test a data pipeline in CI? medium

Unit-test transform functions on fixtures (edge cases), run GE/dbt data tests on sample outputs, and integration-test the pipeline end-to-end on representative data with reconciliation assertions. Idempotency test: run twice, expect identical output.

Why promote the same artifact instead of rebuilding per env? medium

To guarantee prod runs exactly what QA/UAT validated — rebuilding risks dependency drift ("works in QA, breaks in prod"). Build once, promote the versioned artifact, change only config per env.

How do you handle DB/schema changes safely? medium

Schema-as-code with Liquibase/Flyway (versioned, reviewable migrations), backward-compatible/additive changes first, applied through the same CD pipeline, with a rollback migration. For Delta, use schema evolution + expectations.

Docker for Data Engineers

Why it exists, how DEs actually use it, and the VM-vs-container answer interviewers want.

Why Docker exists & Docker vs VM

Docker packages an app + its exact dependencies into an image that runs identically anywhere — solving "works on my machine." A container is a running instance. Vs a VM: a VM virtualizes the whole OS (heavy, minutes to boot, GBs); a container shares the host kernel and isolates only the app (lightweight, seconds, MBs). So containers give reproducibility and density without VM overhead.

How DEs use Docker

  • Local dev parity — spin up Postgres, Kafka, Airflow, Spark locally via docker-compose to mirror prod.
  • Containerized jobs — package a PySpark/Python job + deps as an image, run on Kubernetes/OpenShift or as a container task (reproducible, no cluster dependency drift).
  • Airflow — run Airflow itself in containers; the KubernetesPodOperator launches each task as a container (isolated deps).
  • dbt — containerize dbt runs for consistent, CI/CD-deployed transformations.
  • Kafka/connectors — run brokers/connect workers as containers in dev/test.
  • CI — tests run inside containers so the CI environment exactly matches.
  • Kubernetes / OpenShift — orchestrate containers at scale (scaling, self-healing, scheduling); OpenShift is enterprise Kubernetes with added security/governance.
Interview line: "Docker gives environment reproducibility — the exact Python/Spark/library versions ship with the code, so a job runs the same on my laptop, CI, and prod. Unlike a VM it shares the host kernel, so it's lightweight and fast. We containerize jobs and run them on Kubernetes, and use compose to stand up Kafka/Airflow/Postgres locally."

Interview Q&A

Image vs container? basic

Image = immutable built template (layers). Container = running instance of an image. Build once, run many.

When Kubernetes over just running containers? medium

When you need orchestration at scale: scheduling, autoscaling, self-healing, rolling deploys, service discovery. For a single job a container is enough; for many services/jobs with reliability needs, Kubernetes (or OpenShift in enterprises) manages them.

Terraform & Infrastructure as Code

How an entire secure Azure data environment gets created from code — repeatably, across every environment.

Why IaC

Manually clicking infrastructure ("click-ops") is unrepeatable, undocumented, drift-prone, and impossible to review. Terraform declares infrastructure as versioned code: you write what you want, Terraform figures out the changes (plan) and applies them (apply), tracking real state in a remote state file. The same code + per-env variables creates identical dev/qa/uat/prod environments, reviewed via PR like any code.

What Terraform provisions for a data platform

│   ├── databricks/    # workspace, cluster policies, secret scope, UC resources

│   ├── keyvault/      # vault + access policies/RBAC
│   ├── datafactory/   # ADF + linked services + managed IR

The whole landing zone from the Azure chapter — storage, VNet-injected Databricks, Key Vault, Private Endpoints, RBAC role assignments, ADF, monitoring — is code. Spin up a new environment in minutes; tear down cleanly; every change is reviewed and auditable.

  • State — stored remotely (Azure Storage backend) with locking so the team doesn't clobber each other.
  • Modules — reusable components (a "storage" module used by every project).

Interview Q&A

What problem does IaC solve? basic

Repeatability, reviewability, and drift control. Environments are identical and reproducible, changes are version-controlled and peer-reviewed, and you can rebuild/rollback infra reliably — impossible with manual clicking.

What is Terraform state and why does it matter? medium

State maps your code to real resources so Terraform knows what exists and what to change. Store it remotely with locking (shared, safe for teams); losing/corrupting it or editing by hand causes drift and dangerous applies.

How do you keep dev and prod consistent? medium

Same Terraform modules across all envs; only per-env tfvars differ (sizing, HA, policy strictness). Same principle as app code: identical logic, config-only differences, promoted through CI.

Production Issues Playbook

The cross-project incident runbook. For each: the symptom, the likely cause, and how you diagnose & fix it. This is where your Jio production-support background becomes your biggest interview weapon.

The incident method (say this before any specific fix)

Detect (alert/monitor) → Triage (severity, blast radius, is data wrong or just late?) → Localize (which source/stage/partition — compare source→bronze→silver→gold counts, read logs/Spark UI/lineage) → Mitigate (stop the bleeding: pause, fail-open, hold publish) → Fix root cause → Recover (idempotent, partition-scoped backfill) → Prevent (add a check/alert) → Communicate (stakeholders, RCA writeup). Interviewers want this structured calm, not a random fix.

The incidents (symptom → cause → fix)

Pipeline failure / job crash medium

Check the error + logs + Spark UI. Transient (network/cluster) → retry (idempotent, so safe). Systemic (bad code/data/schema) → fix and reprocess the affected partition. Prevent with retries, alerting, and better validation. Always confirm no partial/duplicate writes — idempotent design makes reruns clean.

Late-arriving data medium

Streaming: watermark accepts bounded lateness; very late → correction path. Batch: reprocess a lookback window (e.g., last 3 days) idempotently so corrections land without duplicates. Sensors detect non-arrival and alert before downstream runs on incomplete data.

Schema change / drift from source hard

Enforce a data contract / schema validation at ingest so a breaking change is quarantined + alerted, not silently corrupting downstream. Additive changes handled via schema evolution. Coordinate change management with the source team. Fix, then reprocess quarantined data.

Duplicate data medium

Root cause: at-least-once delivery, retries, or a fan-out join. Dedup via MERGE on the true key; monitor dedup ratios. Fix the fan-out (know join cardinality) rather than masking with DISTINCT. Idempotent writes prevent recurrence.

Corrupted / malformed files medium

Validate at intake; quarantine the bad file with a reason and alert the provider; never let it corrupt bronze. Reprocess once corrected. Bronze immutability means you can always replay clean.

Storage full / quota hit medium

Immediate: clear temp/checkpoint bloat, expand quota. Root cause: missing VACUUM/retention, small-files explosion, or runaway logs. Prevent with lifecycle/tiering policies, compaction, VACUUM schedules, and storage alerts.

Cluster crash / driver or executor failure hard

Executor loss → Spark recomputes lost partitions from lineage (fault-tolerant); repeated loss = OOM/skew (fix below). Driver failure → job dies (driver holds the DAG/collects); avoid collect() on big data, size the driver, and for streaming rely on checkpoint recovery. Autoscaling + retries handle transient node loss.

Kafka consumer lag medium

Consumers can't keep up. Scale consumers up to partition count, add partitions, speed processing, tune batch size, fix slow sinks. Watch lag as the primary streaming SLO; frequent rebalances (timeouts) also cause it — tune session/poll timeouts and use cooperative rebalancing.

Slow Spark job hard

Spark UI → longest stage. Skew (straggler) → salt/AQE/broadcast. Big shuffle → filter earlier, broadcast small side, pre-partition. Spill → more partitions/memory. Tiny tasks → coalesce. Python UDF → replace with native. Reading too much → partition/column pruning. Fix the biggest bottleneck, measure, repeat.

Partition skew hard

A few keys hold most rows → one task runs forever. Enable AQE skew handling, salt the hot key (random suffix → join → aggregate), broadcast the small side, or isolate the hot key. The tell is a single long-running task in the UI while others finish.

Out of memory (OOM) hard

Causes: too few partitions (huge partitions), skew, collect()/toPandas on big data, wide transforms, caching too much. Fix: increase/rebalance partitions, remove collect, avoid unnecessary caching, raise executor memory sensibly, handle skew. Read spill/GC in the UI.

Permission / access issues medium

Usually a missing RBAC grant, expired secret/SP, or a changed Managed Identity. Check the exact denied resource, verify the identity's role assignments and UC grants, and confirm Key Vault access. Fix via IaC (so it's reproducible), not a manual click.

CI/CD failure medium

Read the failing stage: test failure (fix code/data), quality-gate fail (SonarQube — address issues), Terraform plan conflict (state drift — reconcile), or deploy failure (env/config). Don't bypass gates; fix forward or revert the PR.

Bad deploy → rollback & hotfix hard

Rollback = redeploy the previous versioned artifact (and Terraform revert if infra changed); for streaming, use blue/green with a new checkpoint. Hotfix = minimal fix via the normal pipeline (fast-tracked with approval), never a manual prod edit. Delta time travel can restore a table to a pre-bad-run version.

Disaster recovery (region/platform outage) hard

Defined RTO/RPO drive it: geo-redundant storage, cross-region replication of critical data, IaC to rebuild the environment quickly in another region, and Kafka/CDC replay to reconstruct state (RPO≈0 for replayable sources). Test the DR runbook periodically — an untested DR plan is a wish.

How to use this in interviews: when asked "tell me about a production issue," pick one of these, wrap it in the incident method, and anchor it to a real Jio incident. The structure (detect→localize→mitigate→fix→recover→prevent) is what makes you sound experienced.

  • "Walk me through the architecture." — source→ingest→layers→serve, cadence, key tech + why.
  • "Why did you choose X over Y?" — always have the alternative and the trade-off (see the tech chapters).
  • "How did you ensure data quality/correctness?" — DQ dimensions, checks placement, reconciliation.
  • "What was the hardest bug/incident?" — incident method + a real story.
  • "How does it scale / what breaks at 10×?" — skew, small files, shuffle, serving load, cost.
  • "How is it deployed and monitored?" — CI/CD flow, IaC, the monitoring signals.
  • "How do you handle failures/reprocessing?" — idempotency, backfills, checkpoints, DR.
  • "What would you do differently?" — one honest improvement (shows reflection).
